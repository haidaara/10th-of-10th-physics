The Core Idea

The goal is to analyze the stability of a plasma. We start with a stable, uniform, equilibrium state and then introduce a very small perturbation (a little wave) to see if it grows, decays, or oscillates.

Because the perturbation is small, we can simplify the complex, non-linear Vlasov equation by keeping only terms that are first-order in these small quantities. This is the essence of linearization.

4. Expand the Terms:

  • Time Derivative:

    4. −em(E⃗1)⋅(∇vf1)-\frac{e}{m} (\vec{E}_1) \cdot (\nabla_v f_1)−me​(E1​)⋅(∇v​f1​)

5. Apply the "Small Perturbation" Rule (Linearization):
This is the key. We now classify each term by its order of smallness:

  • Zeroth-order terms: Contain only equilibrium quantities (f0f_0f0​, E⃗0\vec{E}_0E0​, B⃗0\vec{B}_0B0​). These must satisfy the equilibrium on their own.
  • First-order terms: Contain only one small perturbed quantity (e.g., f1f_1f1​, E⃗1\vec{E}_1E1​, or B⃗1\vec{B}_1B1​).
  • Second-order terms: Contain the product of two small perturbed quantities (e.g., E⃗1⋅∇vf1\vec{E}_1 \cdot \nabla_v f_1E1​⋅∇v​f1​ or v⃗×B⃗1⋅∇vf1\vec{v} \times \vec{B}_1 \cdot \nabla_v f_1v×B1​⋅∇v​f1​).

Since f1f_1f1​, E⃗1\vec{E}_1E1​, and B⃗1\vec{B}_1B1​ are all very small, their product is extremely small. We neglect these higher-order terms.

Let's sort our 8 terms:

  • Zeroth-order: Terms 1 and 5.
    −em(E⃗0)⋅(∇vf0)−em(v⃗×B⃗0)⋅(∇vf0)-\frac{e}{m} (\vec{E}_0) \cdot (\nabla_v f_0) -\frac{e}{m} (\vec{v} \times \vec{B}_0) \cdot (\nabla_v f_0)−me​(E0​)⋅(∇v​f0​)−me​(v×B0​)⋅(∇v​f0​)
    These describe the equilibrium and sum to zero.
  • First-order: Terms 2, 3, 6, and 7.
    −em(E⃗0)⋅(∇vf1)−em(E⃗1)⋅(∇vf0)−em(v⃗×B⃗0)⋅(∇vf1)−em(v⃗×B⃗1)⋅(∇vf0)-\frac{e}{m} (\vec{E}_0) \cdot (\nabla_v f_1) -\frac{e}{m} (\vec{E}_1) \cdot (\nabla_v f_0) -\frac{e}{m} (\vec{v} \times \vec{B}_0) \cdot (\nabla_v f_1) -\frac{e}{m} (\vec{v} \times \vec{B}_1) \cdot (\nabla_v f_0)−me​(E0​)⋅(∇v​f1​)−me​(E1​)⋅(∇v​f0​)−me​(v×B0​)⋅(∇v​f1​)−me​(v×B1​)⋅(∇v​f0​)

If we assume E⃗0=0\vec{E}_0 = 0E0​=0 (a common case), the first-order terms simplify further.

6. Write the Final Linearized Vlasov Equation:
After canceling the zeroth-order terms and dropping the second-order terms, we are left with:

∂f1∂t+v⃗⋅∇rf1−em(v⃗×B⃗0)⋅∇vf1=em(E⃗1+v⃗×B⃗1)⋅∇vf0\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial t} + \vec{v} \cdot \nabla_r f_1 - \frac{e}{m} (\vec{v} \times \vec{B}_0) \cdot \nabla_v f_1 = \frac{e}{m} (\vec{E}_1 + \vec{v} \times \vec{B}_1) \cdot \nabla_v f_0∂t∂f1​​+v⋅∇r​f1​−me​(v×B0​)⋅∇v​f1​=me​(E1​+v×B1​)⋅∇v​f0​

7. Assume a Wave Solution (Normal Mode Analysis):
To solve this linear PDE, we assume the solution for the perturbation is a plane wave:
f1(r⃗,v⃗,t)=f1(v⃗) ei(k⃗⋅r⃗−ωt)f_1 (\vec{r}, \vec{v}, t) = f_1(\vec{v}) \, e^{i(\vec{k} \cdot \vec{r} - \omega t)}f1​(r,v,t)=f1​(v)ei(k⋅r−ωt)
This converts the differential operators into algebraic multipliers:

  • ∂∂t→−iω\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \rightarrow -i\omega∂t∂​→−iω

Summary

The linearization process boils down to:

  1. Split quantities into big equilibrium + small perturbation.
  2. Substitute into the original equation.
  3. Expand and Classify terms by their order (zeroth, first, second).
  4. Neglect second-order terms (∝(small)2\propto \text{(small)}^2∝(small)2).
  5. Cancel the zeroth-order terms (they define the unperturbed equilibrium).
  6. The remaining first-order terms form the Linearized Vlasov Equation.
  7. Assume a wave form to turn calculus into algebra.

This resulting equation is now solvable and forms the basis for calculating dispersion relations for waves in plasma, like Langmuir waves or Alfvén waves.
